Research Statement

In metal additive manufacturing (MAM), the metal (typically in powder form) is repeated melted by an energy source (e.g., laser) and solidified (when disengaged from the laser) to become the 3-dimensional build. My research is to simulate the physical phenomena in this process with computational techniques. The simualtion, by "reproducing" the physical phenomena, can contribute to the maturation of MAM in two ways: (1) the underlying science can be quantitatively understood, which informs better process design. (2) the simulation can partially replace the trial-and-error tests to expedite the optimization of process parameters.

Background

1. Process Physics

Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAM) is an emerging manufacturing technique which has the potentail to revolutionalize the manufaturing industry credited to its capabilities of producing parts with flexbile geometries. Typically, an energy source (laser, electron beam, etc.) melts metal powders to form a molten pool. When the laser is disengaged from the metal, the molten pool cools down to form the solid part. This process can be repeated layer-by-layer, and only the powders undergoing the melting-solification process will contribute to the bulk part. Therefore, the build geometry can be easily manipulated by controlling the interaction between the laser and the powders.

Despite the straightforward concept of MAM, the underlying physics in a typical MAM process is not fully understood. Recently, in-situ observations have revealed the complexity of the multi-physcis phenomena.

Let's first consider the interaction of laser and a bare metal plate (no powder involved). This "simpler" setup resembles that of a laser welding process. The in-situ high-speed X-ray imaging technique reveals more details on the multiphysics phenemena, specificaly, the metal vaporization and the formation of keyhole.

When a layer of powder is deposited on the metal substrate, the laser-metal interaction is further complicated. The metal vaporization form a vapor jet which then entrains the surrounding powders towards the laser-illuminated region. This is known as a classic fluid mechanics phenomenon referred to as the "entrainment". Once entrained, the powders have the chance of being incorporated into the molten pool or ejected away by the vapor jet.

Moving on to the case where the laser can be moved relative to the metal. The laser heating still triggers the metal vaporization and the formation of keyhole. However, the keyhole dynamics is more complicated than the stationary laser cases. The "front keyhole wall" refers to the inclined keyhole surface which is relatively stable. The "rear keyhole wall" is the keyhole surface that is dynamically oscillating, displaying drastically different dynamics than the front keyhole wall. At this point of time, this dynamic behavior is not fully understood, but is crutial to the development of powder bed fusion process.

The process physics of MAM is an active ongoing research area. The state-of-the-art experimental and mutliphysics simulation techniques keep revealing these physical phenomena at both finer spatial and temporal scale. The development and certification of MAM will be highly dependent on these scientific findings.

2. Structural Features (Grain Structure)

Due to the unique process physics, the thermal history that the metal material experiences in MAM is also unique. This thermal history then determines the distinctive microstructure of the metal upon its solidification. Specifically, "exotic" grain structures have been identified for a variety of alloy materials. E.g., alluminum based (Al-10Si-Mg), iron based (stainless steel AISI 316), and titanium based (Ti-6Al-4V) alloys. The uniqueness of the thermal history of MAM lies in that the solidification rate is extremly fast and directional. Since the laser only heats a specific location for a short period of time (~ tens of micronseconds), the metal material experiences a surge of temperature (melting and vaporization) followed by a rapid solidification which follows the direction of the laser. Indeed, it is the fast and directional solidification that causes the unique grain structures in MAM.

Powder Bed Generation and Ray Tracing

This project is aimed to set the foundations for the subsequent multiphyscis simulations for MAM processes. In this project, a powder bed generation model is used to create the structure of a powder bed as in LPBF processes. Then the initial laser-metal interaction, i.e., right when the laser is turned on, is simulated using the ray-tracing algorithm. The laser absorption distribution on the powder surfaces is computed, which will trigger the melting and vaporization of the metal.

A randomly packed powder bed is generated by a "rain-dropping" algorithm (Jodrey, W., et al. Simulation (1979)). The powder bed is then represented by the level-set function. Based on the level-set field, the ray-tracing algorithm is implemented to track the mutliple reflections of laser beam on powder surfaces, which eventually provides the absorption distribution.
